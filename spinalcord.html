The Spinal Cord

Anatomy

The spinal cord is the main transmitter for information from the brain to the nervous system. The spinal cord lies inside the spinal coloumn and originates from the brain stem to the fibrous extension known as the filum terminale. The spinal cord is about 43-45 cm in length depending on gender. The spinal cord is proteted by three layers of meninges; dura mater, arachnoid mater, and pia mater. The spinal cord is ellipitical, and mainly divided into 4 areas; Cervical, Thoraric, Lumbar, and Sacral.

Segments

  • 8 cervical segments forming 8 pairs of cervical nerves
  • 12 thoracic segments forming 12 pairs of thoracic nerves
  • 5 lumbar segments forming 5 pairs of lumbar nerves
  • 5 sacral segments forming 5 pairs of sacral nerves
  • 1 coccygeal segment

Repair

The spinal cord was earlier belived to be irreperable, as the slightest impact with neurons can cause immense pain and disabilities. The spinal cord connects the body’s nerve cells to the brain. Where these neurons connect with the spinal cord, they become motor roots or sensory roots. In an injury, these roots get torn, and connections between certain parts of the body are nil. The method surgeons have created is cutting out the original sensory cells from the root and planting the root deeper in the spinal cord, the dorsal horn; an area filled with more sensory neurons that do not usually connect directly to sensory roots. This is but a small step in repairing a spinal cord, but the only one we have currently.
